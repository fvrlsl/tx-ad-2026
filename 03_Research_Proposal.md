### 1. Background and Motivation

Biocatalysis is rapidly becoming a cornerstone of sustainable chemical manufacturing, offering high selectivity, mild conditions, and reduced waste compared to traditional metal catalysis. However, two persistent bottlenecks limit industrial adoption: **(i) the operational stability of natural enzymes under non-physiological conditions**, and **(ii) the limited substrate scope of any single biocatalyst**. Nanozymes — engineered nanomaterials that mimic enzymatic activity — have emerged as robust, low-cost catalysts that complement these weaknesses but typically lack the exquisite stereo- and chemoselectivity of true enzymes [1,2].

I argue that **the next leap in green biocatalysis lies not in choosing between enzymes and nanozymes, but in rationally coupling them into hybrid catalytic systems**. My own work — a first-author *Chemical Engineering Journal* paper (IF 13.3) on a CuIn₂S₄ peroxidase-mimetic nanozyme [3], and a first-author *International Journal of Biological Macromolecules* paper (IF 8.2) on dynamic covalent hyaluronic acid hydrogels for biomolecule encapsulation [4] — has given me direct experience with both the inorganic and the biological halves of this problem.

This proposal outlines a 3.5-year PhD project to engineer **a modular nanozyme–enzyme hybrid platform** for cascade biocatalysis, with the long-term vision of converting low-value feedstocks into pharmaceutical or fine-chemical building blocks under continuous-flow conditions.

### 2. Central Hypothesis

> *Spatially co-localizing a redox-active nanozyme (e.g., CuIn₂S₄ or a UPO-mimetic Mn-oxide) with an engineered oxidoreductase on a tunable hydrogel scaffold will create a synergistic catalytic node in which the nanozyme regenerates the cofactor / activates O₂ in situ, while the enzyme delivers stereoselective product formation — yielding higher total turnover than either catalyst alone.*

### 3. Specific Aims

**Aim 1 — Hydrogel scaffold engineering.**
Design dynamic covalent hyaluronic-acid / silk-fibroin hybrid hydrogels with tunable porosity, charge density, and degradation kinetics to host both nanozyme and enzyme components. Building directly on my IJBM 2024 work [4].

**Aim 2 — Nanozyme synthesis and computational rationalization.**
Synthesize and benchmark a panel of metal-sulfide nanozymes (CuIn₂S₄, Bi₂S₃, MoS₂) and Mn/Fe-oxide nanozymes targeting peroxidase- and UPO-mimetic activity. Use **Gaussian DFT calculations** to map the active-site electronic structure and predict substrate binding — extending the workflow I developed for my CEJ 2024 paper [3].

**Aim 3 — Enzyme partner selection and immobilization.**
Select 2–3 oxidoreductases (e.g., unspecific peroxygenase UPO, glucose oxidase, alcohol dehydrogenase) and immobilize them within the hydrogel scaffold via dynamic covalent or anchor-peptide chemistry. Characterize loading, leaching, and retained activity.

**Aim 4 — Cascade performance under flow conditions.**
Assemble the hybrid platform into a packed-bed or membrane-flow reactor and benchmark steady-state productivity for a model oxidation cascade (e.g., alcohol → carbonyl → carboxylate). Quantify total turnover number (TTN), space-time yield (STY), and operational lifetime under industrially relevant conditions.

**Aim 5 — Data-driven process monitoring.**
Use **R-based multivariate analysis** to fit kinetic models, deconvolute UV/fluorescence signals, and build a real-time predictive model of bioreactor performance — directly addressing the data-driven monitoring focus of the ELEGANCE DC8 project at TU Delft.
